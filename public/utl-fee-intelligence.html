MEV: The Fee Most Devs Don't Discuss

Maximal Extractable Value is the most costly and least understood drain on any DeFi platform. It happens at the mempool level — before your transaction even confirms. Most platforms don't mention it because they can't stop it. UTL routes around it.

WITHOUT UTL — WHAT'S HAPPENING TO YOUR USERS
The Sandwich Attack Sequence
A bot detects your user's pending swap in the public mempool. It places a buy order in front (front-run), waits for your user's trade to push the price up, then immediately sells (back-run). Your user pays more. The bot profits. You know nothing.
1. User submits: swap 1,000 USDT → KENO at market price
2. Bot detects in mempool — buys KENO first, pushing price up 0.6%
3. User's tx executes at inflated price — pays $6 extra per $1K
4. Bot sells immediately — pockets the difference
5. Result: User is $6–$20 poorer. Bot is richer. You earned nothing.
WITH UTL — HOW THE PROTOCOL RESPONDS
UTL's Internal Routing Architecture
UTL Protocol's transaction routing compresses the MEV window and redirects extractable value back into the FeeCollector. Instead of bots capturing the spread, the ecosystem captures it. Value that was leaving the system stays inside it.
1. User submits through UTL-aware interface with tight slippage
2. Transaction bundled to minimize mempool exposure window
3. Any extractable spread captured by FeeCollector contract
4. FeeCollector routes: 60% stakers / 25% T.D.I.R. / 15% Treasury
5. Result: Same trade, but value stays in the ecosystem.
